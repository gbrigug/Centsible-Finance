Overview

So, what is credit? Credit is an agreement made by two parties. One party agrees to give the other money up front, with the understanding that that money will be repaid over a set amount of time. The individual loaning the money has the potential to profit off the promise - often through fees or interest. The benefit for the person receiving the loaned money is the ability to get the money instantly.

The Importance of Credit

Why is credit important? Well, today’s American economy runs on credit. It is so integrated into every aspect of the financial world. Credit is not just a business related term - everyday individuals interact with credit all the time. If you understand credit - its advantages and risks - you’ll be better off financially.

Debit vs. Credit

You probably get asked this question all the time at the store: debit or credit? It’s a simple question that essentially means: whose really paying for this right now? If you’re using your Discover card, Discover Bank is fronting the cash to get you those nice new Nikes. They expect, of course, they you’ll pay them back in full when you get your statement. However, if you’re using your debit card, you are paying for those Nikes up front - with your own money.

Financial Products

To continue with this dialogue, the amounts of credit based products that exist in an everyday individual’s life is quite astounding. The obvious place to begin is with credit cards. A credit card is a type of relationship that a person has with a bank or credit union. A consumer makes purchases via that card - using the bank or credit union’s money. However, at the end of the month, they are responsible for repaying the sum of all their purchases back to that financial institution.

There are many other types of credit based products. As most individuals do not have enough cash on hand to buy a house outright, they take out a mortgage to buy a home. Similar to a credit card, the borrower gets to use the bank’s money to buy something. However, they aren’t locked into that month payback period. Often, mortgages last tens of years. During this time, the bank profits from that initial loan by charging interest to the borrower. A $200k mortgage may end up costing the borrower a total of $225k by the end of his/her repayment; the devil is in the details.

All of these items rely on credit:

  • Credit cards
  • Cash advances
  • Mortgages
  • Auto loans
  • Student loans
  • Personal loans
  • Payday loans
  • Business loans
